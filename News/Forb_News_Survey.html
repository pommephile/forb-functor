Seven Domains, Four Theorems, One Functor: The Forb Research Program

In under a week, the Forbidden Pattern Functor has generated six papers, ten solved open problems, seven empirical domain verifications, and a categorical unification with the Mandelbrot set. This is the state of the program.
June 25, 2026

Research programs sometimes unfold slowly, paper by paper over years. Sometimes they explode. The Forbidden Pattern Functor — a universal algorithm for extracting Boolean constraints from any symbol system — belongs to the second category. In the span of a few days, it has produced a body of work spanning mathematical foundations, theoretical computer science, number theory, physics, linguistics, and neuroscience.

"Forb began as a chemical curiosity — 17 fragment types that never appear together in 123 million compounds. It turned out to be a universal mathematical operation hiding in plain sight."

The Architecture of the Program

The research program has a clear internal structure. The foundation paper establishes Forb as a mathematical object with seven theorems proving it is a well-behaved functor. The survey maps the landscape. Four independent papers apply Forb to specific domains — logic (PA/Gödel), physics (5-layer tower), machine learning (LLM constraints), and number theory (Mandelbrot unification). Two News & Views articles — on topological grammar and on feature dimension — provide accessible entry points. All papers are cross-referenced, and the survey contains a dependency graph showing which results depend on which others.

Publication roadmap. Six papers at arXiv-ready quality. The foundation paper, three independent papers (PA, LLM, Tower), the unification paper (OP10+OP11), and the survey. All have passed internal structural review. The survey recommends submission order: Foundation → Unification → PA → Tower → LLM → Survey (synthesis last).
