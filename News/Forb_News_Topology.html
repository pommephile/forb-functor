Chemistry Speaks in Loops, Language in Voids

Betti numbers of forbidden-pattern spaces reveal that chemical and linguistic grammars occupy complementary topological dimensions — a discovery with implications for how nature encodes constraints.
June 25, 2026

When chemists enumerate which molecular fragments can follow which others, and linguists catalog which sounds can follow which in English, they are doing the same thing: extracting constraints from absence. But the shape of those constraints, it turns out, is fundamentally different.

"Chemical grammar is 1-dimensional — it's about which paths exist. Linguistic grammar is 2-dimensional — it's about which triangles are hollow."

UOTA applied the Forbidden Pattern Functor — a universal algorithm for extracting Boolean constraints from any symbol system — to two real-world domains: the BOC/CLOC chemical database (123 million compounds) and the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary (134,000 English words). The topological analysis of the resulting constraint spaces revealed a striking complementarity.

Loops vs. Voids

For chemistry (111 allowed molecular fragment types in a 7-dimensional hypercube), the constraint space has 238 independent cycles — different "routes" through chemical space that can't be reduced to each other — but zero 2-dimensional voids. The hypercube is bipartite: no three fragments can all be pairwise adjacent, so no "hollow triangles" can form.

For English phonotactics (39 phonemes), the picture inverts. The constraint space has zero independent cycles — all 1-dimensional loops are filled in — but 7,130 independent 2-dimensional voids. These voids are concrete: they represent triplets of phonemes like {AA, AH, ER} where any two can appear together (AA-AH appears 13 times, AH-ER appears 812 times, AA-ER appears 273 times) but all three never appear in sequence in any English word.

Key numbers. Only 249 bigrams (16.4%) are forbidden in English, yet they generate 7,130 independent 2D voids — a 28.6-fold amplification. A small set of constraints creates an enormously complex void structure.

Why This Matters

The finding suggests that the "dimension" of a grammar — whether its complexity is primarily 1-dimensional (cycles) or 2-dimensional (voids) — may be a fundamental property of the domain. Chemical constraints are inherently local (what can follow what), while phonological constraints involve higher-order interactions (which triples are excluded even when all pairs are allowed).

The same method applies to any domain with a symbol inventory and a corpus: nuclear physics (20,886 nuclide pairs), particle physics (30 Standard Model particles), protein domain architectures, and undeciphered scripts. The Betti numbers of the constraint space provide an objective, domain-independent measure of "grammar shape."

Scope note. This work establishes topological signatures of constraint spaces. It does not claim that chemical or linguistic grammars are defined by these signatures — only that the Forb functor reveals them as objective mathematical properties of the corpus. Cross-linguistic validation across non-English phonologies has not been performed.
