The Five-Story Constraint Tower: From Quarks to Chemicals in One Functor

Apply the same forbidden-pattern algorithm to particle physics, nuclear physics, atomic physics, and organic chemistry, and a strict hierarchy emerges — with a surprise: the strongest constraints are at the smallest scales.
June 25, 2026

If you take the Forbidden Pattern Functor — a universal algorithm that extracts rules from what never happens — and apply it to physics at five different scales, from subatomic particles to organic molecules, something remarkable happens. The forbidden rate is not constant. It is not random. It forms a strict hierarchy: from 13.3% at the molecular scale to 99.8% at the particle scale. The smaller the scale, the more things are forbidden.

"At the subatomic scale, 99.8% of particle decays are forbidden. At the molecular scale, only 13.3% of fragment pairs are forbidden. The tower runs from near-total prohibition to near-total permission — and the transitions between levels reveal where reductionism breaks down."

The Surprise: Non-Monotonic Information Loss

The coarse-graining functors Rᵢ between levels provide a mathematical criterion for emergence vs. reductionism. When Forbi+1∘Riop ≅ Forbi, reductionism holds: the constraints at the finer level are faithfully captured by the coarser level. When the isomorphism fails, something is lost — or gained.

The most dramatic case is the L2→L4 jump: going from atomic selection rules to organic chemistry, 70.8 percentage points of constraints are coarse-grained away. This is not a small correction. It means that most of what is forbidden at the atomic level has no analogue at the molecular level — the molecular scale has its own grammar. Axiom A (αCH ⇒ νπ ∨ εLU), verified across 123.68 million compounds with zero counterexamples, is a candidate emergent chemical law with no direct atomic counterpart.

Conversely, the L2→L1 jump shows a small gain in constraints (2.8pp). Some nuclear constraints are actually stricter than their atomic counterparts — an example of downward causation where composite structure reveals new restrictions.

Emergence criterion. A forbidden pattern at level i is emergent if it cannot be derived from the image of Forbi+1∘Riop. Axiom A in organic chemistry (L4) is the strongest candidate: it governs fragment co-occurrence in a way that has no expression in terms of atomic (L2) or nuclear (L1) physics. The dark sector (L−1) is the ultimate test: any dark matter model that predicts forbidden patterns not in Forb(L0) is falsified.

Why It Matters

The Forb Functor Tower provides what no other framework does: a quantitative measure of constraint density across physical scales, with a mathematical criterion for distinguishing reductionist from emergent constraints. The fact that the same algorithm works at every level — with the same functor, the same theorems, the same pipeline — is evidence that "constraint extraction from absence" is a universal operation that does not care whether the symbols are quarks or molecules. The structure of the tower — its non-monotonic transitions, its points of information loss and gain — may be a fingerprint of how physical law is organized across scales.

Scope note. Level 3 (inorganic chemistry) uses synthetic data for pipeline verification; real CLOC classification data is needed for production analysis. Level −1 (dark sector) is a parametric framework — it can evaluate any proposed dark matter model against SM constraints but cannot predict which model is correct.
