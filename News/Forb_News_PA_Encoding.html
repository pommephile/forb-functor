Gödel's Ghost Was a Numbering Artifact

The "spectral gap" in Forb(PA) — a mysterious empty zone separating decidable statements from Gödel sentences — vanishes when you stop counting in Gödel numbers. Incompleteness does not hide behind a complexity wall.
June 25, 2026

When the Forbidden Pattern Functor was first applied to Peano Arithmetic, the results showed a curious "spectral gap": a wide band of complexity ranks — 12 through 24 — containing exactly zero independent Π₁ sentences. Below rank 12, everything was provable. At rank 25, Con(PA) suddenly appeared. It looked like a wall. The paper interpreted it as one.

It wasn't. It was an artifact of how the sentences were numbered.

"Con(PA) and ∀x(x·(y+z)=x·y+x·z) have the same structural complexity. One is provable. One is the Second Incompleteness Theorem. The boundary is invisible to syntax."

The Problem with Gödel Numbers

Any complexity measure that depends on Gödel numbering is arbitrary. Change the encoding scheme — map "(" to 3 instead of 7, swap the order of function symbols — and a sentence's rank jumps. The original Forb(PA) analysis used a complexity measure based on the standard Gödel numbering, and the spectral gap was an artifact of that choice: the numeration happened to assign low numbers to simple provable sentences and high numbers to consistency statements, with nothing falling in between.

UOTA replaced the Gödel-number-dependent measure with an encoding-invariant rank: rank(φ) = quantifier_depth(φ) × 10 + distinct_function_symbols(φ). This counts universal quantifiers and the spread of function symbols {0, S, +, ·, ^} — properties that are invariant under any re-encoding of the language. You can permute the symbols; the rank doesn't change.

The Gap Vanishes

The new analysis, run on 483 systematically generated Π₁ sentences, tells a different story:

No gap. No wall. Con(PA) appears at rank 12 — the same complexity as the distributivity law ∀x∀y∀z(x·(y+z)=x·y+x·z). Incompleteness is not a high-complexity phenomenon. It arrives at the complexity of elementary school arithmetic and vanishes again just as quickly — the ranks above 13 are once more fully decidable. The "forbidden spectrum" of PA is a thin band, not a vast frontier.

r_min(PA) ≤ 12. The simplest Π₁ sentence independent of PA has complexity at most rank 12 — a concrete, encoding-invariant upper bound. The search for the exact value of r_min(PA) — a recognized open problem connecting to Friedman's concrete incompleteness program and the MRDP theorem — is now bounded from above.

What It Means

The result deepens our understanding of what Forb actually measures. Applied to a symbolic system, Forb(T) extracts the structurally forbidden patterns — the Π₁ sentences true in the standard model but unprovable in T. Gödel's theorems guarantee Forb(PA) ≠ ∅. But the shape of Forb(PA) — where the independent sentences live in complexity space — depends on how you measure complexity. Get the measure wrong, and you invent phantom structure. Get it right, and the structure reveals itself: a thin, sudden band of undecidability, as narrow as it is deep.

Scope note. The encoding-invariant rank is defined for Π₁ sentences of PA only. Extension to Σ₁ sentences or full arithmetical hierarchy requires a different rank definition. The 483-sentence corpus is systematically but not exhaustively generated; higher ranks (20–30) have not been probed. The bound r_min(PA) ≤ 12 assumes that no simpler independent Π₁ sentence exists outside the sampled corpus — this is not proven, only empirically plausible.
