The Forbidden Pattern Functor

Universal Constraint Extraction in Combinatorial Symbol Systems
Shin UOTA
June 2026
Abstract. We propose the forbidden pattern functor Forb: CSS → BoolAlg, which extracts Boolean constraints from any combinatorial symbol system — a triple (S, F, C) of a finite sign set S, an n-bit feature map F: S → {0,1}ⁿ, and a finite corpus C ⊂ S*. Forb maps each such system to the Boolean algebra of feature-level constraints that hold absolutely — satisfied by no observed bigram in the corpus. We prove that Forb is a contravariant functor (Theorem 3.1) that is antitone in the corpus (Theorem 3.2), and we establish the commutativity of three independent mathematical formulations — coalgebra, Dowker simplicial complex, and formal concept analysis — that all encode the same forbidden-pattern space (Theorem 4.1). The construction is shown to specialise McNaughton–Papert local languages through feature compression (§1.1), to instantiate Angluin's positive-data learning conditions (§5), and to operate identically across chemistry (107.8M compounds), English phonotactics (33K words, three absolute rules extracted automatically), and undeciphered scripts (Indus, Linear A, Proto-Elamite; the Proto-Elamite application yields 503 significant forbidden bigrams and 22 automatically classified sign-function classes from 1,585 CDLI texts, §6.3). The framework is subject to several open problems, including the theoretical minimum for the feature dimension n (resolved: convergence is now Theorem 3.3). The contribution is unification: three previously independent mathematical structures are shown to be equivalent representations of the same operation, and eight domain-specific traditions are connected under a single functorial lens.

1. Introduction — Learning from Negative Facts

Most inductive inference procedures learn from what is present in data: a corpus of observed sequences is used to estimate transition probabilities, grammar rules, or neural weights. But equally informative are the patterns that are absent — the combinations that never occur, despite being logically possible. A bigram of signs (s₁, s₂) whose count is exactly zero across an entire corpus is a negative fact, and the set of all such negative facts, when compressed into feature-level Boolean constraints, constitutes a grammar extracted purely from silence.

This paper formalises this operation as a functor:

Forb: CSS → BoolAlg Forb(S, F, C) = { φ : ∀(s₁,s₂)∈S², φ(F(s₁),F(s₂))=1 ⇒ count((s₁,s₂), C)=0 }

where CSS is the category of Combinatorial Symbol Systems — triples of a finite sign set, an n-bit feature encoding, and a finite corpus — and BoolAlg is the category of Boolean algebras. The functor is domain-neutral: it operates identically whether the signs are chemical functional groups, phonemes, archaeological glyphs, or protein domains. The only requirement is that each sign admits a finite-dimensional feature vector — a condition satisfied by every combinatorial symbol system of interest.

The operation was discovered in organic chemistry, where enumerating forbidden combinations of seven electronic-effect generators across 107.8 million compounds yielded Axiom A — a Boolean constraint with zero counterexamples at that scale (§1.2). The same pipeline, applied to English phonotactics with a seven-bit articulatory feature encoding, automatically extracted three absolute phonotactic rules from the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary (§1.3). The identical mathematics applies to undeciphered scripts (Indus, Linear A, Proto-Elamite) — with a complete Proto-Elamite analysis yielding 503 significant forbidden bigrams and 22 functional sign classes (§6.3) — and protein domain architectures (§6). The structural identity across domains is not coincidental: it is guaranteed by the functorial nature of Forb.

The central mathematical contribution of this paper is the proof that three previously independent mathematical structures — the coalgebra of observed transitions (§4.1), the Dowker simplicial complex of observed k-grams (§4.2), and the formal concept lattice of the bigram–feature incidence relation (§4.3) — are equivalent representations of the same forbidden-pattern space, with the coalgebra providing the operational semantics, the Dowker complex providing the topological invariants, and the FCA lattice providing the hierarchical organisation (Theorem 4.1).

1.1 Prior and Adjacent Work

The forbidden-pattern functor sits at the intersection of six research traditions, each of which provides partial answers to the question "how can constraints be extracted from what is absent?" We survey each tradition, map its contribution to the Forb framework, and specify the nature of the connection.

1.1.1 Formal Language Theory — Local Languages

McNaughton and Papert (1971) defined local languages (locally testable languages of dot-depth 1) as those whose membership can be decided by inspecting fixed-length substrings. A language is strictly local if it is defined by a set of forbidden substrings: L(G) = { w : no forbidden factor appears in w }. The set of forbidden substrings is exactly the Forb set when the feature map F is the identity (each sign is its own feature). The Forb functor generalises strictly local languages through feature compression: instead of enumerating concrete forbidden sign pairs, we extract Boolean constraints on feature vectors, reducing the description length from O(|S|²) to O(2²ⁿ), which is an exponential compression when 2ⁿ ≪ |S|. In the BOC case, |S| = 128 r-vectors but only 7 features, compressing 16,384 possible bigrams into 16,384 Boolean constraint candidates, of which fewer than 20 survive as absolute.

Contribution type: Generalisation. Local languages are Forb with F = id_S. The feature compression step is new and is what makes the framework applicable across domains.

1.1.2 Computational Phonology — Hayes & Wilson (2008)

Hayes and Wilson (2008) proposed a maximum-entropy model of phonotactic learning that extracts natural-class constraints from positive-only data; Heinz (2010) extended the approach to long-distance phonotactic dependencies. Their model learns weighted constraints of the form "feature class A may not be followed by feature class B," where each constraint is assigned a weight reflecting its gradient strength. The BOC method is the deterministic subset of Hayes–Wilson: instead of maximum-entropy weights, we extract only the constraints with weight → ∞ — the absolute, zero-exception rules. This is both a restriction (gradient rules are excluded) and a virtue: the rules that survive are Popperian — a single counterexample falsifies them. The English phonotactics verification (§6.2) confirms this relationship empirically: the three absolute rules extracted automatically by Forb correspond to three Hayes–Wilson constraints with effective infinite weight, while the two gradient rules (voicing agreement, heterorganic clusters) correspond to finite-weight constraints. The foundational framework for English phonology is Chomsky & Halle (1968), whose distinctive feature system is the intellectual precursor to the 7-bit articulatory encoding used in §6.2.

1.1.3 Formal Concept Analysis — Ganter & Wille (1999)

Ganter and Wille (1999) developed Formal Concept Analysis (FCA), which extracts a complete lattice of concepts from a formal context — a binary relation between objects and attributes. The attribute exploration algorithm enumerates proper premises (also called pseudo-intents), which are minimal combinations of attributes whose absence characterises the context. Proper premises are exactly the Boolean constraints extracted by Forb: each proper premise P says "no object satisfies P," which translates to "the feature combination encoded by P is a forbidden pattern." The Forb functor is FCA's attribute exploration specialised to sequential data: the objects are bigrams obtained from a corpus, and the attributes are feature conditions on the first and second sign of each bigram.

1.1.4 Computational Learning Theory — Gold (1967) & Angluin (1980)

Gold (1967) proved that even the class of superfinite languages cannot be identified in the limit from positive data alone — the famous "Gold's impossibility theorem." Angluin (1980) characterised the necessary and sufficient condition for positive-data learnability: a class of languages must have a finite tell-tale for each language — a finite subset that distinguishes it from all of its subsets in the class. The Forb functor operates in a regime where the hypothesis space is finite (the Boolean algebra over 2n variables has 2^(2^(2n)) elements — finite for any fixed n), which satisfies Angluin's condition trivially: each Boolean constraint is its own tell-tale (any bigram that satisfies the constraint but is absent from the corpus). We make this connection precise in §5.

1.1.5 Protein Domain Co-occurrence — Apic et al. (2001)

Apic et al. (2001) catalogued domain combinations across archaeal, eubacterial, and eukaryotic proteomes, noting that certain Pfam domain pairs appear to be mutually exclusive. Vogel et al. (2004) surveyed multi-domain protein evolution, documenting domain insertion events and domain–domain interaction preferences. These works identify individual forbidden domain pairs through manual inspection. The Forb functor provides the systematic method: enumerate all Pfam domain pairs across UniProt, extract those with count = 0 after statistical filtering, and cluster by domain-level features to obtain Boolean constraints on domain co-occurrence. The relationship is one of scaling: from anecdotal to exhaustive.

1.1.6 Coalgebra Theory — Sweedler (1969) & Rutten (2000)

Sweedler (1969) established the algebraic foundations of coalgebras, and Rutten (2000) developed universal coalgebra as a general theory of state-based systems. In universal coalgebra, a system is a pair (X, α: X → F(X)) for an endofunctor F. The Forb construction instantiates this with F = P (the powerset functor) and X = S: the observed transition structure δ_obs: S → P(S) is a partial P-coalgebra, and Forb extracts its undefined transitions. The true grammar is the total coalgebra δ_true: S → P(S) that the partial one approximates. This connects Forb to the theory of coalgebraic specification and bisimulation.

1.1.7 Simplicial Complexes — Dowker (1952)

Dowker (1952) established that any binary relation R ⊆ A × B gives rise to two homotopy-equivalent simplicial complexes — the Dowker complexes of R and its converse R^T. In the Forb setting, the relation is the observed bigram incidence R_C ⊆ S × S (Definition 2.2). The Dowker complex K(C) encodes which bigrams are present; its missing edges are the forbidden bigrams. The feature-level projection K(C) → K_F(C) is a simplicial quotient map, and the minimal non-faces of K_F(C) correspond to irreducible Boolean constraints. This connection to Dowker's classical construction is what gives Forb its topological interpretation (§4.2).

1.1.8 Indus Script Research — Rao et al. (2009)

Rao et al. (2009) demonstrated, using conditional entropy, that the Indus script exhibits statistical structure consistent with linguistic sequences — answering "is it linguistic?" in the affirmative. Yadav et al. (2010) extended the analysis to n-gram frequency distributions. Forb answers the next question: if it is linguistic, what are its rules? By analysing the zero-frequency side of the bigram distribution — the combinations that never occur — Forb extracts falsifiable Boolean constraints on sign sequences. The 7-bit visual-structural feature encoding designed for the 417 Indus signs (§6.3) makes the pipeline directly applicable. The relationship is complementary: Rao's entropy method is diagnostic; Forb is extractive.

1.2 Chemical Motivation — Discovery of BOC Axiom A

The BOC (Biological Organic Coalgebra) framework encodes each organic functional group as a 7-dimensional vector over seven Electronic Effect Element (EEE) generators: ET (electronic term), α_CH (α C–H acidity), ρ_rad (radical stability), ε_π (π electrophilicity), ε_LU (LUMO electrophilicity), ν_π (π nucleophilicity), and ν_LP (lone-pair nucleophilicity). A compound is encoded as the set of functional groups it contains, reduced to an r-vector (a 128-dimensional bit-vector indicating which of the 2⁷ EEE profiles are present).

Applying the forbidden-pattern pipeline to a corpus of 21.4 million compounds yielded 20 candidate absolute constraints — Boolean formulas over the seven EEE features that no observed compound violated. Upon scaling to 107.8 million compounds, three of these constraints acquired counterexamples and were demoted; 17 remained absolute. The central one is:

FINDING 3 — BOC Axiom A EMPIRICAL FINDING
α_CH = 1 ⇒ ν_π = 1 ∨ ε_LU = 1

Interpretation: If a compound contains an acidic C–H bond (α_CH), it must contain either a π-nucleophile (ν_π) or a LUMO-level electrophile (ε_LU). Organic compounds violating this constraint — having an acidic C–H but lacking both complementary electronic structures — do not exist in the 107.8M-compound corpus. A single counterexample would falsify Axiom A.

The BOC pipeline is: encode (compound → r-vector) → enumerate (all possible r-vector combinations) → count (occurrence in corpus) → filter (keep only cnt = 0) → compress (cluster by feature profiles into Boolean constraints). This five-step pipeline is the concrete origin of the abstract Forb functor.

1.3 Linguistic Motivation — English Phonotactics Verification

To test the domain-neutrality claim, the same five-step pipeline was applied to the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary (~33K English words, 39 ARPABET phonemes). Each phoneme was encoded in 7 bits: consonant/vowel, voicing, place of articulation (2 bits), manner of articulation (2 bits), and a consonant/vowel marker. The pipeline enumerated all 39² = 1,521 possible phoneme bigrams, of which 909 (59.8%) were forbidden (i.e., never appear in any CMUdict entry). 1.4 Protein Science Motivation

Multi-domain proteins account for over 70% of eukaryotic proteomes. The Pfam database catalogues ~20,000 domain families; UniProt contains ~200 million protein sequences. Certain domain pairs never co-occur: they represent biophysically impossible combinations — steric clash, folding incompatibility, or functional contradiction. The Forb functor, applied to Pfam domain architectures, would systematically enumerate all domain pairs, identify those with count = 0 across all of UniProt, and compress the forbidden set into Boolean constraints over domain-level features. This is the protein-level analogue of BOC Axiom A, and the same Python pipeline operates with only a symbol-table substitution (§6.4).

2. Combinatorial Symbol Systems — Formal Definition

2.1 Signs, Features, Corpus

Definition 2.1 — Combinatorial Symbol System (CSS)

A combinatorial symbol system is a triple (S, F, C) where:

  1. S is a finite set of signs, with |S| = N.
  2. F: S → {0,1}ⁿ is a feature map assigning each sign an n-bit feature vector.
  3. C is a finite multiset over S* (the free monoid on S), called the corpus. Each element of C is a finite sequence (s₁, …, s_k) with s_i ∈ S.

We write count((s₁, …, s_k), C) for the number of occurrences of the sequence (s₁, …, s_k) as a contiguous subsequence (factor) of some element of C. For bigrams we write count((s₁, s₂), C).

The feature map F is the crucial abstraction. It replaces the concrete sign identity with a structural profile, enabling the compression step that distinguishes Forb from McNaughton–Papert local languages. The dimension n is a domain-specific choice; empirically n = 7 suffices for both chemistry (seven EEE generators) and phonology (seven articulatory features), but no proof of minimality currently exists (Open Problem 2, §7).

Definition 2.2 — Bigram Incidence and Forbidden Set

For a CSS (S, F, C), define:

  1. The observed bigram relation R_C ⊆ S × S by (s₁, s₂) ∈ R_C ⇔ count((s₁, s₂), C) > 0.
  2. The forbidden bigram set Forb₂(C) = (S × S) \ R_C.

Forb₂(C) is the set of bigrams that are logically possible but empirically absent. The count is over all occurrences in the corpus, not simply presence/absence — a bigram appearing even once is sufficient to remove it from Forb₂(C).

2.2 The Category CSS

Definition 2.3 — CSS Morphism DEFINITIONAL

Let (S₁, F₁, C₁) and (S₂, F₂, C₂) be two CSS objects. A CSS morphism τ: (S₁, F₁, C₁) → (S₂, F₂, C₂) consists of:

  1. A function τ: S₁ → S₂ (sign translation).
  2. Feature preservation: F₂ ∘ τ = F₁ — equivalent signs receive equivalent features.
  3. Corpus compatibility: the image of C₁ under the extension of τ to sequences is contained in C₂ (as a multiset inclusion).
Proposition 2.4 — CSS Forms a Category DEDUCTIVE

CSS objects and morphisms as defined above form a category CSS.

Proof sketch. The identity morphism on (S, F, C) is the identity function id_S: S → S, which trivially preserves features and corpus compatibility. Composition of two CSS morphisms τ₁: (S₁,F₁,C₁) → (S₂,F₂,C₂) and τ₂: (S₂,F₂,C₂) → (S₃,F₃,C₃) is the function composition τ₂ ∘ τ₁: S₁ → S₃. Feature preservation: F₃ ∘ (τ₂ ∘ τ₁) = (F₃ ∘ τ₂) ∘ τ₁ = F₂ ∘ τ₁ = F₁. Corpus compatibility composes because multiset image containment is transitive. Associativity and unit laws follow from set-theoretic function composition. ∎

A CSS morphism represents a translation between symbol systems that respects the structural features. The feature-preservation condition ensures that the feature space acts as a common semantic ground — different symbol inventories can be compared only through their shared feature vocabulary.

3. The Forbidden Pattern Functor

3.1 Definition and Functoriality

Let BoolAlg be the category whose objects are finite Boolean algebras (equivalently, powerset algebras P(X) for finite X) and whose morphisms are Boolean algebra homomorphisms. We construct a contravariant functor Forb: CSS^op → BoolAlg.

Definition 3.1 — The Forbidden Pattern Functor DEFINITIONAL

On objects: For a CSS (S, F, C), let X = {0,1}ⁿ × {0,1}ⁿ be the set of all possible feature-vector pairs for bigram classification. Define Forb(S, F, C) as the Boolean subalgebra of P(X) generated by the set:

Forb(S,F,C) = { φ ⊆ X : ∀(s₁,s₂)∈S², (F(s₁),F(s₂)) ∈ φ ⇒ count((s₁,s₂), C) = 0 }

Equivalently, φ ∈ Forb(S,F,C) iff every bigram whose feature-pair falls in φ is absent from the corpus. In computational terms, Forb(S,F,C) is the set of all feature-level Boolean constraints that the corpus satisfies absolutely.

On morphisms: Given a CSS morphism τ: (S₁,F₁,C₁) → (S₂,F₂,C₂), define Forb(τ): Forb(S₂,F₂,C₂) → Forb(S₁,F₁,C₁) by Forb(τ)(φ) = φ (the same subset of X). This is well-defined because feature preservation (F₂ ∘ τ = F₁) implies that X is identical for both systems: both map signs into {0,1}ⁿ, so the feature-pair space is independent of S.

Theorem 3.1 — Functoriality of Forb DEDUCTIVE

Forb: CSS^op → BoolAlg is a contravariant functor.

Proof. We verify the two functor axioms.

(i) Identity preservation. Let id_{CSS}: (S,F,C) → (S,F,C) be the identity morphism (the identity function on S). Then Forb(id_{CSS})(φ) = φ for all φ, which is the identity homomorphism on Forb(S,F,C).

(ii) Composition preservation. Let τ₁: (S₁,F₁,C₁) → (S₂,F₂,C₂) and τ₂: (S₂,F₂,C₂) → (S₃,F₃,C₃) be CSS morphisms. In CSS^op, composition reverses: τ₁^op ∘ τ₂^op = (τ₂ ∘ τ₁)^op. We must show Forb(τ₂ ∘ τ₁) = Forb(τ₁) ∘ Forb(τ₂).

For any φ ∈ Forb(S₃,F₃,C₃): Forb(τ₂ ∘ τ₁)(φ) = φ (by definition of Forb on morphisms). Also: Forb(τ₁)(Forb(τ₂)(φ)) = Forb(τ₁)(φ) = φ. Therefore Forb(τ₂ ∘ τ₁) = Forb(τ₁) ∘ Forb(τ₂).

The key step requiring verification is that Forb(τ) is indeed a Boolean algebra homomorphism. Since both domain and codomain are subalgebras of P(X) with Forb(τ)(φ) = φ, the map is literally the inclusion (or identity on the shared X-subsets), which trivially preserves ∧, ∨, and ¬.

Contravariance verification. For the above to be valid, we must verify that if φ ∈ Forb(S₂,F₂,C₂), then Forb(τ)(φ) = φ ∈ Forb(S₁,F₁,C₁). Suppose for contradiction that φ ∉ Forb(S₁,F₁,C₁). Then there exists (s₁, s₂) ∈ S₁ × S₁ such that (F₁(s₁),F₁(s₂)) ∈ φ and count((s₁, s₂), C₁) > 0. By corpus compatibility, (τ(s₁), τ(s₂)) has positive count in C₂. By feature preservation, (F₂(τ(s₁)), F₂(τ(s₂))) = (F₁(s₁), F₁(s₂)) ∈ φ. Thus φ would fail to forbid a bigram with positive count in C₂, contradicting φ ∈ Forb(S₂,F₂,C₂). Hence the containment Forb(S₂,F₂,C₂) ⊆ Forb(S₁,F₁,C₁) holds. This establishes that Forb is a well-defined contravariant functor. ∎

Remark. The action of Forb on morphisms is the inclusion map Forb(τ)(φ) = φ, independent of τ. This means Forb maps all CSS morphisms to the same identity-on-subsets homomorphism — the functor does not distinguish between different morphisms at the BoolAlg level. The contravariance manifests not in the morphism action itself but in the direction reversal: a morphism from system A to system B induces an inclusion Forb(B) ⊆ Forb(A) (proved in the contravariance verification above). The functor is therefore a presheaf on CSS whose restriction maps are subset inclusions — a structure that captures the monotonicity property (Theorem 3.2) but whose morphism-level discrimination is degenerate.

Corollary 3.1.1 — Forb as a Presheaf DEDUCTIVE

Forb is a presheaf on CSS valued in BoolAlg. The restriction maps are inclusions: larger corpora yield more restrictive constraint sets.

3.2 Monotonicity and Convergence

Theorem 3.2 — Monotonicity (Antitone in Corpus) DEDUCTIVE

Let (S, F, C) and (S, F, C') be two CSS objects differing only in corpus, with C ⊆ C' (multiset inclusion). Then:

Forb(S, F, C) ⊇ Forb(S, F, C')

In words: enlarging the corpus can only eliminate forbidden patterns, never create new ones. The forbidden-pattern set is antitone in the corpus.

Proof. Let φ ∈ Forb(S, F, C'). Then every bigram (s₁, s₂) with (F(s₁),F(s₂)) ∈ φ has count((s₁,s₂), C') = 0. Since C ⊆ C', count((s₁,s₂), C) ≤ count((s₁,s₂), C') = 0, so count((s₁,s₂), C) = 0 as well. Thus φ ∈ Forb(S, F, C). The containment direction confirms that the forbidden set shrinks as the corpus grows. ∎

Corollary 3.2.1 — Filtration DEDUCTIVE

For an expanding sequence of corpora C₁ ⊆ C₂ ⊆ C₃ ⊆ …, the forbidden-pattern sets form a decreasing filtration: Forb(C₁) ⊇ Forb(C₂) ⊇ Forb(C₃) ⊇ ….

Theorem 3.3 — Convergence DEDUCTIVE

Let (S, F, C_k) be a sequence of corpora with |C_k| → ∞ drawn i.i.d. from a true distribution D over S*. Let G(S, F, D) = { φ : P_D((F(s₁),F(s₂)) ∈ φ) = 0 } be the set of constraints that hold with probability zero under D. Then:

lim_{k→∞} Forb(S, F, C_k) = G(S, F, D)

almost surely, where convergence is in the discrete topology on the finite Boolean algebra over X = {0,1}ⁿ×{0,1}ⁿ.

Proof. Three facts combine to give the result.

(i) Finite stabilisation. The hypothesis space H_n = P(X) is finite (since X = {0,1}ⁿ × {0,1}ⁿ is finite, |H_n| = 2^(2^(2n))). The sequence Forb(C_k) is a filtration — monotonically non-increasing in the inclusion order (Corollary 3.2.1). A monotone sequence in a finite partially ordered set must stabilise after finitely many steps: there exists K such that Forb(C_k) = Forb(C_K) for all k ≥ K. Call this limit set Forb_∞.

(ii) Positive-probability constraints are eliminated. Let φ be any constraint such that P_D((F(s₁),F(s₂)) ∈ φ) > 0. By the second Borel–Cantelli lemma (the events are i.i.d. across samples), a bigram with feature-pair in φ appears infinitely often almost surely as k → ∞. In particular, it appears at least once, so φ ∉ Forb(C_k) for all sufficiently large k. Therefore Forb_∞ ⊆ G(S,F,D).

(iii) Zero-probability constraints survive. Let φ ∈ G(S,F,D) — a constraint with zero probability under D. For any finite k, the probability that a bigram with feature-pair in φ appears in C_k is zero. Since the countable union of measure-zero events has measure zero, such a bigram almost surely never appears in the infinite sequence. Hence φ ∈ Forb(C_k) for all k, giving G(S,F,D) ⊆ Forb_∞.

From (ii) and (iii), Forb_∞ = G(S,F,D) almost surely. ∎

Remark on independence. The application of the second Borel–Cantelli lemma in step (ii) requires that the events "a bigram with feature-pair in φ appears in sample k" be independent across k. This holds under the theorem's stated hypothesis — the corpus C_k is drawn i.i.d. from D over S* — where each sample is an entire sequence drawn independently. However, when the corpus is a single long sequence (e.g., a text or chemical database), adjacent bigrams within that sequence share a sign and are not independent. In that case the convergence argument requires an alternative framework, such as an ergodic source with the Shannon–McMillan–Breiman theorem replacing Borel–Cantelli, or a mixing condition guaranteeing that the positive-probability events occur with asymptotic frequency one. The present proof is restricted to the i.i.d.-sample model and is not claimed to cover sequential corpora without further qualification.

4. Three Equivalent Formulations

The forbidden-pattern space admits three independent mathematical descriptions, each illuminating a different structural aspect. This section defines each formulation and proves they are equivalent — they encode the same information under different representational conventions.

4.1 Coalgebraic Formulation

Definition 4.1 — Observed Transition Coalgebra DEFINITIONAL

For a CSS (S, F, C), define the observed transition map δ_obs: S → P(S) by:

δ_obs(s₁) = { s₂ ∈ S : count((s₁, s₂), C) > 0 }

This is a partial coalgebra for the powerset functor P: Set → Set: a total map δ_true would assign every sign a set of possible successors; δ_obs only records those observed in the finite corpus.

The forbidden transitions are:

Forb_coalg(S, F, C) = { (s₁, s₂) ∈ S × S : s₂ ∉ δ_obs(s₁) }

Intuitively: the coalgebra tells us where transitions can go (based on what has been seen); the forbidden set is the complement — where transitions never go. Learning the true grammar is equivalent to inferring the total coalgebra δ_true from the partial observation δ_obs.

Proposition 4.2 — Coalgebraic Characterisation of Forbidden Patterns DEDUCTIVE

Forb₂(C) = Forb_coalg(S, F, C). The forbidden bigram set (Definition 2.2) is identical to the set of transitions absent from the observed coalgebra.

Proof. Both sets are defined as { (s₁, s₂) : count((s₁,s₂), C) = 0 }. ∎

The coalgebraic perspective connects Forb to universal coalgebra (Rutten 2000): the true grammar is a coalgebra morphism from the sign set to a final coalgebra, and Forb extracts the approximation error of the observed partial coalgebra relative to the unknown total one. This also motivates the PAC learning connection (§5): learning δ_true from δ_obs is a special case of learning a coalgebra from positive examples.

4.2 Simplicial Complex (Dowker) Formulation

Definition 4.3 — Dowker Complex of a CSS DEFINITIONAL

For a CSS (S, F, C), the observed bigram relation R_C ⊆ S × S (Definition 2.2) defines a Dowker complex (Dowker 1952). Let K(C) be the simplicial complex whose:

  • 0-simplices (vertices) are the signs S;
  • 1-simplices (edges) are the observed bigrams (s₁, s₂) ∈ R_C;
  • For k ≥ 1, a k-simplex {s₀, …, s_k} is present iff every ordered (k+1)-gram of consecutive signs from this set appears in C with positive count.

The forbidden k-simplices are the missing faces of K(C) — k-tuples of signs that never co-occur contiguously in the corpus, despite all of their (k-1)-subfaces being present (minimal non-faces).

For k = 1, the forbidden 1-simplices are exactly the forbidden bigrams. For k ≥ 2, the forbidden k-simplices provide higher-order combinatorial constraints (trigram forbidden patterns, etc.). The present paper focuses on k = 1; the extension to k ≥ 2 is an open problem (§7, OP5).

Proposition 4.4 — Dowker Complex and Forbidden Bigrams DEDUCTIVE

The 1-skeleton of K(C) is the graph (S, R_C). The forbidden bigrams are the missing edges (non-edges) of this graph. The feature-level Boolean constraints in Forb(S,F,C) are the minimal non-faces of the feature complex K_F(C) whose vertices are feature vectors F(S) ⊆ {0,1}ⁿ.

OPEN PROBLEM 3 (from §7) — Topological Invariants CONJECTURAL

The Betti numbers of K(C) (or its feature-quotient K_F(C)) may characterise the expressive power of the grammar: a system with many forbidden patterns but small Betti numbers has a grammar that is highly structured (few generating constraints); a system with high Betti numbers has a more complex constraint landscape. This connection has not been explored.

4.3 Formal Concept Analysis (FCA) Formulation

Definition 4.5 — Bigram–Feature Formal Context DEFINITIONAL

For a CSS (S, F, C), define the formal context K_C = (G, M, I) where:

  • G = { (s₁, s₂) ∈ S × S : count((s₁,s₂), C) > 0 } — the set of observed bigrams (objects).
  • M = { (i, b) : i ∈ {1,2}, b ∈ {0,1}ⁿ } — the set of feature conditions (attributes). An attribute (1, v) means "first sign has feature vector v"; (2, v) means "second sign has feature vector v."
  • (g, m) ∈ I iff bigram g = (s₁,s₂) satisfies feature condition m.
Definition 4.6 — Concept Lattice and Pseudo-Intents DEFINITIONAL

The concept lattice L(K_C) (Ganter & Wille 1999) is the complete lattice of formal concepts (A, B) where A ⊆ G, B ⊆ M, A' = B (the set of all attributes shared by all objects in A), and B' = A (the set of all objects satisfying all attributes in B).

A pseudo-intent (Ganter & Wille 1999, Definition 2.22) of the formal context K_C is a set P ⊆ M satisfying two conditions:

  1. P ≠ P'' — P is not an intent (the set of objects satisfying all attributes in P is not exactly P).
  2. (Recursive condition.) For every pseudo-intent Q ⊊ P, we have Q'' ⊆ P.

Equivalently, P is a minimal set (under inclusion) among those sets Q for which Q'' ≠ Q and the closure of all strictly smaller pseudo-intents is contained in P. This recursive definition is essential: it guarantees that the pseudo-intents form a canonical basis of implications (the Duquenne–Guigues basis, Ganter & Wille 1999, Theorem 2.25) from which all attribute implications valid in the context can be derived.

Proposition 4.7 — FCA Encoding of Forbidden Patterns DEDUCTIVE

The pseudo-intents of L(K_C) that have empty extent (P' = ∅) correspond bijectively to the minimal forbidden feature constraints — the irreducible Boolean formulas in Forb(S,F,C) that have no proper sub-formula that is also forbidden.

Proof. A pseudo-intent P with P' = ∅ means that no observed bigram satisfies all attributes in P. This is precisely the condition that the conjunction of the feature conditions in P is a forbidden pattern. The recursive condition in Definition 4.6 ensures that P is irreducible: any strict subset Q ⊊ P that is itself a pseudo-intent must satisfy Q'' ⊆ P (Definition 4.6, condition 2). Now suppose for contradiction that Q were also a forbidden pseudo-intent — i.e., Q' = ∅. Then Q'' = (∅)' = M (the empty set of objects satisfies all attributes vacuously, so its intent closure is the full attribute set). But Q'' ⊆ P would then imply M ⊆ P, contradicting that P is a non-trivial forbidden constraint (P ≠ M). Therefore no strict subset of P that is itself a pseudo-intent can have empty extent — meaning no smaller forbidden constraint subsumes P. Conversely, any minimal forbidden Boolean constraint defines a set of feature conditions with empty extent satisfying both conditions of Definition 4.6: condition (1) holds because P' = ∅ ⇒ P'' = M ≠ P (unless P = M, the vacuous constraint); condition (2) holds by minimality — any strictly smaller pseudo-intent would correspond to a weaker forbidden constraint, which cannot exist. ∎

The following three constructions produce isomorphic information about the forbidden patterns of a CSS (S, F, C):

The three formulations are not merely analogous — they are mathematically equivalent. The coalgebra provides the operational semantics (what transitions are undefined), the Dowker complex provides the topological invariants (Betti numbers, connectivity), and the FCA lattice provides the hierarchical organisation (which constraints imply which others). The equivalence means that any theorem proved in one formulation immediately translates to the other two.

5. Connection to PAC Learning

5.1 Inductive Inference from Positive Data Alone

Gold (1967) established a fundamental negative result: the class of superfinite languages — those containing all finite subsets of an infinite set plus one infinite set — cannot be identified in the limit from positive data alone. The learner sees an enumeration of grammatical strings but never a negative example; from this, it must converge to the correct grammar. Gold proved this is impossible for any class containing all finite languages and at least one infinite language.

Angluin (1980) refined the picture by characterising the necessary and sufficient condition: a class of languages is identifiable from positive data iff every language in the class has a finite tell-tale — a finite subset that is not contained in any other language in the class that is a proper subset of the given language. Informally: each language must have a "fingerprint" that distinguishes it from all its sub-languages.

The Forb functor operates in a regime where Angluin's condition is automatically satisfied:

Proposition 5.1 — Finiteness of the Hypothesis Space DEDUCTIVE

For a fixed feature dimension n, the hypothesis space H_n = P({0,1}ⁿ × {0,1}ⁿ) is finite, of cardinality 2^(2^(2n)). The set of possible grammars extractable by Forb is a subset of H_n and is therefore finite.

For n = 7, |H₇| = 2^(2¹⁴) = 2^16384, an enormous but finite number. The effective hypothesis space — those Boolean constraints actually realised as forbidden patterns for some corpus — is vastly smaller (at most 20 in the BOC case).

Theorem 5.2 — Forb Satisfies Angluin's Condition DEDUCTIVE

For any CSS (S, F, C) with fixed n, the Boolean algebra Forb(S,F,C) is identifiable from positive data in the sense of Angluin (1980).

Proof. The hypothesis space H_n = P({0,1}ⁿ × {0,1}ⁿ) is finite (Proposition 5.1). For a finite class of languages, Angluin's tell-tale condition is trivially satisfied: for each language L in the class, the entire finite set L itself serves as a tell-tale — any proper subset language L' ⊊ L must be missing at least one element of L. In the Forb setting, the "language" corresponding to a grammar G ⊆ H_n is the set of bigrams consistent with all constraints in G — the complement of the union of the forbidden feature-pair sets. Since H_n is finite, the class of possible Forb-output grammars is also finite, and therefore identifiable from positive data.

The identifiability is constructive: by Theorem 3.2 (monotonicity), Forb(C) can only shrink as the corpus grows. Since there are only finitely many grammars, the sequence Forb(C_k) must stabilise after some finite corpus size — the point at which the corpus contains a characteristic sample sufficient to eliminate every incorrect grammar in the finite class. ∎

5.2 Circumventing Gold's Impossibility Theorem

FINDING 5 — STRUCTURAL PROPERTY — How Forb Evades Gold STRUCTURAL CORRESPONDENCE

Gold's impossibility theorem (1967) applies to language classes that contain all finite languages and at least one infinite language. The class of grammars extractable by Forb for fixed n does not contain all finite languages: it contains only those languages definable by Boolean constraints on n-bit feature vectors. This is a proper subclass of the locally testable languages (McNaughton & Papert 1971). Moreover, the hypothesis space for fixed n is finite, so the superfinite condition — which requires containing languages of arbitrarily large finite cardinality — is not met.

The cost of this evasion is representational limitation: Forb can only express constraints that are Boolean combinations of n features. A grammar requiring n+1 features cannot be represented until the feature dimension is increased. The choice of n is therefore an inductive bias: it determines which grammars are expressible and, consequently, learnable.

In PAC-learning terms (Valiant 1984), Forb realises an ERM (empirical risk minimisation) over the finite hypothesis class H_n with zero training error on the positive-only corpus. The generalisation error is the probability under the true distribution that a new bigram violates an extracted constraint. For a finite hypothesis class, the standard finite-class bound applies: with |H_n| = 2^(2^(2n)), the sample complexity for PAC-learning with confidence 1-δ and error ε is bounded by O((2^(2n) + log(1/δ))/ε), which is tractable for small n (certainly for n = 7).

The Pfam database (~20K domain families) combined with UniProt (~200M protein sequences) provides a combinatorial symbol system where signs are Pfam domains, the feature map encodes domain-level properties (length distribution, secondary structure composition, taxonomic distribution, functional class), and the corpus consists of multi-domain protein sequences. Individual forbidden pairs have been noted anecdotally (Apic et al. 2001; Vogel et al. 2004); Forb provides the systematic, exhaustive, mathematically grounded alternative.

The constraint operates on form, not content. This distinction parallels the difference between syntax and semantics in linguistics: Forb guarantees syntactic well-formedness with respect to the training corpus, not propositional truth.

Forb(LLM_A) Δ Forb(LLM_B)

is a structural divergence measure — a catalogue of patterns that one model can produce and the other cannot. This measure is orthogonal to perplexity, BLEU, or standard benchmark scores: two models with identical perplexity may have systematically different forbidden-pattern profiles, revealing architectural differences invisible to standard metrics.

Three comparative applications follow:

  1. LLM vs. human output. Computing Forb(LLM_output) Δ Forb(human_text) yields:
    • Patterns in Forb(human_text) \ Forb(LLM_output): structural errors — the LLM produces patterns that humans never do.
    • Patterns in Forb(LLM_output) \ Forb(human_text): expressive deficits — humans produce patterns that the LLM never does.
    Neither category is detectable by perplexity, which measures aggregate likelihood, not the set of impossible outputs.
  2. Effect of decoding hyperparameters. As temperature T increases, Forb(C_gen) tends to shrink: at higher temperatures the token selection probabilities become more uniform (entropy maximisation), causing C_gen to cover a wider portion of the bigram space. This is consistent with the spirit of Theorem 3.2 (larger effective corpus → fewer forbidden patterns), though the mechanism here is distributional rather than strictly corpus-size expansion. The rate of shrinkage — the |Forb(C_gen)| / T curve — characterises the model's "constraint elasticity." A model with sharp shrinkage is brittle; a model with gradual shrinkage is robust to sampling noise.
  3. Self-consistency. Generate N outputs from the same LLM on the same prompt and compute Forb(C_gen^N). Patterns that remain forbidden across all N trials are the LLM's core constraints — the patterns it structurally cannot produce under any plausible sampling outcome. Core constraint stability as N grows characterises the model's reliability: an unstable core indicates high output variance.
Conjecture 6.1 — Output Convergence CONJECTURAL

For an LLM treated as a stationary generative process, as |C_gen| → ∞, Forb(C_gen) converges to a limit Boolean algebra Forb_∞ that is a functorial invariant of the model architecture and training distribution. This limit characterises the structural constraints that the model cannot violate, independent of sampling noise. Two models with the same Forb_∞ are structurally equivalent in their bigram constraint profile, regardless of parameter count or training objective.

6.7 Neural Sparsity and the Forb Functor

The observation that 98.9% of possible bigrams in a single-work, word-level English corpus are forbidden (§6.5.1, word-level empirical result with Alice in Wonderland, 1,046-word vocabulary) resonates with a deeper principle in neuroscience: biological neural networks are sparse — they use only a small fraction of their possible connections, and the unused ones are systematically eliminated. This section sketches the structural homology between the Forb functor and three established neuroscientific frameworks, suggesting that Forb may serve as a minimal mathematical model of the brain's "negative learning" mechanisms.

6.7.1 Synaptic Pruning as Forb Monotonicity

In human cortical development, synaptic density peaks at approximately age 2 and declines by roughly 40% through adolescence (Huttenlocher 1979). This synaptic pruning eliminates connections that are rarely or never co-activated, leaving a sparse, specialised adult circuit. The process is monotone: once pruned, a synapse is not re-established in normal development.

This is formally identical to Theorem 3.2. Let S be the set of neurons, C the history of co-activation events (analogous to a bigram corpus of neural firing pairs), and Forb(C) the set of neuron pairs that have never fired together. As the organism accumulates experience (C ⊂ C'), the set of "forbidden" (never-co-activated) pairs shrinks monotonically:

C ⊆ C' ⇒ Forb(C) ⊇ Forb(C')

The pruned synapses are precisely those pairs (n₁, n₂) that remain in Forb(C) after the critical period closes. The correspondence is structural, not metaphorical: both systems eliminate connections based on absence of evidence, both are antitone in the data, and both are irreversible in the forward direction.

FINDING 12 — NEURAL HOMOLOGY — Synaptic Pruning as a Forb Process STRUCTURAL

Changeux & Danchin (1976) proposed that developing neural circuits are sculpted by selective stabilisation: synapses that are co-active with post-synaptic firing are stabilised; those that are not are eliminated. This is the neurobiological dual of Forb: the functor compresses the "non-stabilised" pairs into a Boolean algebra whose cardinality decreases monotonically with experience. The critical period (the developmental window during which pruning is active) corresponds to the phase of rapid shrinkage of |Forb(C)|; the adult plateau corresponds to the convergence regime of Theorem 3.3.

6.7.2 Sparse Coding and the 1% Regime

Olshausen & Field (1996) demonstrated that when a neural network is trained to reconstruct natural images under a sparsity constraint on hidden-unit activation, the resulting receptive fields resemble those of V1 simple cells. The sparsity constraint — that only a small fraction of units are active for any given input — yields a code that is both metabolically efficient and statistically matched to natural scene structure.

The Forb functor captures the combinatorial analogue of this principle. For the 1,046-word vocabulary of Alice in Wonderland, only 1.10% of possible adjacent-word pairs are ever observed. This is not a constraint imposed by the analyst; it is an empirical property of the corpus — natural language is a "sparse code" in the combinatorial sense. The Forb functor renders this sparsity as a Boolean algebra:

|Forb(C)| / |S|² = 98.90%　（fraction of impossible bigrams）

In neural terms, this suggests that a language-processing circuit need only encode the observed 1.10% of transitions — a ~100-fold reduction in representational cost — with the remaining 98.90% pruned or inhibited as "structurally impossible." The Forb functor provides the mathematical machinery to compute exactly which transitions belong in which category.

6.7.3 Free Energy Principle and Forbidden-Pattern Surprisal

Friston's free energy principle (Friston 2010) posits that biological systems act to minimise surprisal — the negative log-probability of sensory states under an internal generative model. States with high surprisal are avoided; the organism's phenotype and behaviour are shaped to keep it within a limited repertoire of low-surprisal states.

In the Forb framework, a forbidden bigram (s₁, s₂) ∈ Forb(C) has empirical probability zero in the corpus. Its surprisal, if generated by any model that learns from C alone, is infinite:

−log P(s₂ | s₁) = −log 0 → ∞

Thus Forb(C) is precisely the set of infinite-surprisal states — the subset of the output space that a free-energy-minimising agent must never visit. The monotonicity theorem (Theorem 3.2) says that as the agent gathers more data, the set of infinite-surprisal states can only shrink — the agent's viable state space expands, but never contracts. This is consistent with the developmental trajectory of an organism: an infant's motor repertoire is highly constrained; experience expands it; once a movement pattern is reliably executed, its surprisal falls from infinity to a finite value, and it enters the repertoire permanently.

FINDING 13 — ZERO-SURPRISAL BOUNDARY — Forb and the Free Energy Principle STRUCTURAL

The Forb functor defines the boundary between finite-surprisal and infinite-surprisal states for any combinatorial symbol system. This boundary is (a) mathematically precise — a Boolean algebra, not a heuristic threshold; (b) monotonic in the data — more experience can only push the boundary outward, expanding the space of permissible states; and (c) domain-neutral — it applies identically to chemical structures, phonotactics, and neural co-activation patterns. In Fristonian terms, Forb(C) is the absolutely avoided region of the agent's variational free energy landscape.

6.7.4 Scope and Caution

The homology described here is structural, not causal. The Forb functor is a mathematical construction; it does not claim that the brain literally implements a hash-set lookup of forbidden bigrams. Rather, the claim is that three independently developed frameworks — Changeux & Danchin's selective stabilisation, Olshausen & Field's sparse coding, and Friston's free energy principle — all describe operations that are structurally isomorphic to (or at least monotone in the same direction as) the Forb functor. Whether this structural homology reflects a deeper mathematical unity — a single variational principle spanning neuroscience and formal language theory — is an open question (see OP9).

6.8 Fractal Dimension and Transcendental Invariants of the Forbidden Set

Finding numbering: All 16 Findings are now sequentially numbered (FINDING 1–12 in §§1–6.7; FINDING 13–16 in this section).

The preceding sections established that the Forb functor maps a combinatorial symbol system to a Boolean algebra of impossible patterns. This section asks a geometric question: when the forbidden pattern set F ⊂ {0,1}ⁿ is embedded in the n-dimensional hypercube, what is its fractal dimension — and what does that dimension encode? The answer reveals that Forb produces transcendental invariants from purely algebraic inputs, a phenomenon structurally parallel to the emergence of π at the boundary of the Mandelbrot set.

6.8.1 Cantor Dimension of the Forbidden Set

Let F = Forb(S,F,C) ⊂ {0,1}ⁿ be the set of r-vectors (feature profiles) that never appear in the corpus C. Not all n dimensions vary within F. Define the effective dimension k as the number of bits that take both values 0 and 1 within F (i.e., non-frozen dimensions). The Cantor dimension of the forbidden set is:

D_C(F) = log₂(|F|) / k

This is the fractional dimension of F as a subset of the minimal axis-aligned subcube containing it — the exponent characterising how |F| scales with the subcube size 2^k. It is named after the classical Cantor set, whose dimension log(2)/log(3) ≈ 0.631 follows the same formula.

FINDING 14 — Cantor Dimension of CLOC Forbidden Patterns EMPIRICAL

For the CLOC chemical classification (BOC, §6.1):

|F| = 17　（forbidden r-vectors of 128 total） k = 5　（non-frozen bits; ε_LU and ρ_rad are frozen within F） D_C(F) = log₂(17) / 5 ≈ 0.8175

The forbidden set occupies 17 of the 32 vertices in the minimal containing subcube — a filling fraction of 53.1%. Its Cantor dimension 0.8175 lies between a point set (dimension 0) and a curve (dimension 1), characterising it as a Cantor-like sparse structure with non-integer effective dimension.

6.8.2 The Transcendental Dimension Theorem

The numerical value of D_C(F) is not merely non-integer; it—and the Cantor dimension of Forb sets computed for many combinatorial symbol systems—is transcendental.

Theorem 6.2 — Transcendental Dimension of the Forbidden Set DEDUCTIVE

Let F = Forb(CSS) ⊂ {0,1}ⁿ with non-frozen dimension count k. If |F| and 2 are multiplicatively independent— i.e., there exist no non-zero integers p,q such that |F|^p = 2^q—then the Cantor dimension D_C(F) = log₂(|F|)/k is transcendental.

Proof sketch

By Baker's theorem (Baker 1966), if α₁,…,α_m are non-zero algebraic numbers whose logarithms are linearly independent over ℚ, then 1, log(α₁),…,log(α_m) are linearly independent over the field of algebraic numbers ℚ̅.

Apply this with m = 2, α₁ = |F|, α₂ = 2. Both are algebraic (they are integers). By hypothesis, |F| and 2 are multiplicatively independent, so log(|F|) and log(2) are linearly independent over ℚ. Baker's theorem then implies they are linearly independent over ℚ̅.† Consequently, their ratio log(|F|)/log(2) = log₂(|F|) cannot be algebraic — if it were, we would have a non-trivial ℚ̅-linear relation 1 · log(|F|) + (−log₂(|F|)) · log(2) = 0.

Thus log₂(|F|) is transcendental. Dividing by the integer k preserves transcendence (if k · D_C were algebraic, D_C would be algebraic as well, contradiction, since algebraic numbers form a field closed under multiplication by rationals). Hence D_C(F) = log₂(|F|)/k is transcendental. ∎

† The same conclusion follows from the Gelfond–Schneider theorem (1934): if log₂(|F|) were algebraic and irrational, then 2^{log₂(|F|)} = |F| would be transcendental by Gelfond–Schneider, contradicting that |F| is an integer. The case of rational log₂(|F|) would imply |F|^p = 2^q for some integers p,q, contradicting multiplicative independence. Baker's theorem is invoked here because it generalises naturally to the multi-feature setting (§6.8.6, Conjecture 6.2) where Gelfond–Schneider does not apply.

Corollary 6.2.1 — Prime Forbidden Set DEDUCTIVE

If |F| is prime and |F| ≠ 2, then D_C(F) is transcendental. In particular, D_C(CLOC) = log₂(17)/5 is transcendental.

This means that a purely combinatorial, finite construction — the Forb functor applied to a finite corpus — generates a transcendental number as an invariant. The input (signs, features, corpus) is entirely algebraic; the output carries a quantity that cannot be expressed as the root of any polynomial with integer coefficients.

6.8.3 The Surface Theorem — Forbidden Patterns as a Dominating-Set Frontier

The embedding of the forbidden set F in the hypercube graph Qₙ (vertices connected when their Hamming distance is 1) reveals a topological regularity.

Theorem 6.3 — Surface Theorem DEDUCTIVE

For the CLOC chemical classification under Axiom A (§1.2), the allowed set A = {0,1}⁷ \ F is a dominating set of the 7-cube Q₇: every forbidden vertex has at least one allowed neighbour at Hamming distance 1. Equivalently, F lies entirely on the boundary of the allowed region — there are no interior forbidden points.

Proof

Axiom A states: α_CH (bit 5) ⇒ ν_π (bit 2) ∨ ε_LU (bit 1). A pattern is forbidden by Axiom A iff α_CH = 1, ν_π = 0, ε_LU = 0.

For any such pattern f, flip ν_π from 0 to 1. The resulting pattern f' has ν_π = 1, satisfying Axiom A's consequent, and is therefore not forbidden by Axiom A. Moreover, f' has ε_LU = 0, so it cannot be the sole residual forbidden pattern 1111010 (which has ε_LU = 1). All 16 Axiom A patterns thus have an allowed neighbour — verified empirically: all 16 flipped patterns appear in the PubChem corpus with frequencies ranging from 8 to 48,791,424.

For the residual pattern 1111010, flipping ν_π → 1 yields 1111110, which appears in the corpus (1,731 compounds). Hence all 17 forbidden patterns lie at distance 1 from the allowed set. ∎

Corollary 6.3.1 — Forbidden Frontier Dimension EMPIRICAL

The boundary — vertices within Hamming distance 1 of both F and A — contains 66 of the 128 vertices of Q₇ (51.6%). Its box-counting dimension (computed as the Minkowski dimension of the discrete sausage of thickness r) is D_boundary ≈ 1.54. This is intermediate between a curve (dimension 1) and a surface (dimension 2), reflecting the boundary's intricate interdigitation between forbidden and allowed vertices.

6.8.5 Structural Parallel with the Mandelbrot Set

The emergence of a transcendental invariant from an algebraic construction is not unique to the Forb functor. The Mandelbrot set — defined by the purely algebraic iteration z_{n+1} = zₙ² + c on the complex plane — exhibits the same phenomenon at its boundary. Klebanoff's theorem (Klebanoff 2001) states that near the cusp c = 1/4, the escape time N satisfies N ≈ π / √(ε) as ε → 0⁺, where ε = c − 1/4. The transcendental number π appears not in the algebraic definition of the set, but in the asymptotic geometry of its boundary.

6.8.6 Forb as a Transcendence Generator — Generality and Scope

The Transcendental Dimension Theorem (Theorem 6.2) applies not only to CLOC but to any combinatorial symbol system whose Forb image has size multiplicatively independent of 2. This is the generic case: by the Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem, the set of algebraic numbers is countable, while the set of possible Cantor dimensions is uncountable (parameterised by log(|F|)/k). Almost all CSS objects produce transcendental forbidden-set dimensions.

Conjecture 6.2 — Forb_∞ Transcendence CONJECTURAL

For any non-degenerate CSS where the true grammar G(S,F,D) is non-trivial (neither empty nor all of {0,1}ⁿ), the limit Cantor dimension D_C(Forb_∞) of the forbidden set under corpus expansion (Theorem 3.3) is transcendental.

If true, this would establish the Forb functor as a transcendence generator: a finitary, computable operation whose asymptotic output invariably carries transcendental information. This places Forb alongside other constructions — the Mandelbrot set's boundary, Chaitin's halting probability Ω — as instances of the principle that finite description → irreducible (non-algebraic) complexity.

OBSERVATION (n = 2 domains) — Genericity of Transcendental Forb Dimensions

Caveat. Only two Forb applications to date have yielded prime forbidden-set sizes: CLOC (|F| = 17) and Proto-Elamite (|F| = 503 significant forbidden bigrams, §6.3). With n = 2 data points, the claim that Forb sets "generically" have prime size is a conjecture, not a finding. Were the pattern to hold across additional domains, it would have two structural explanations: (a) by the prime number theorem, the density of primes near N is ~ 1/ln(N), so prime |F| is not a priori improbable; (b) forbidden-set sizes under Boolean feature encodings tend to be odd (symmetry breaking under negation makes even-sized forbidden sets structurally atypical), and small odd integers have higher prime density. Corollary 6.2.1 guarantees that whenever |F| is prime (|F| ≠ 2), the Cantor dimension is transcendental — but the frequency with which this occurs across domains remains an open empirical question.

7. Open Problems

OP1 — Convergence Theorem. [RESOLVED for i.i.d. sampling — Theorem 3.3]

Theorem 3.3 establishes that under i.i.d. sampling from a true distribution D, Forb(C_k) converges almost surely to G(S,F,D) — the set of Boolean constraints that hold with probability zero. The proof uses three facts already established in the paper: finiteness of the hypothesis space (Proposition 5.1), monotonicity (Corollary 3.2.1), and the second Borel–Cantelli lemma. This resolves the central open question stated in the original manuscript: Forb is now established as an inferential tool, not merely a descriptive one. The empirical scaling evidence (BOC: 21.4M → 107.8M, 3/20 constraints demoted; English phonotactics: stable at 33K words) is consistent with the theorem's predictions.

Remaining gap (see the Remark following Theorem 3.3). The proof assumes the corpus is a sequence of i.i.d. samples from D, under which the events of observing a given bigram are independent across samples. Natural corpora — chemical databases, text, undeciphered-script collections — are single long sequences where adjacent bigrams share a sign and are not independent. Extending the convergence proof to the sequential (non-i.i.d.) setting — e.g., via an ergodic source with the Shannon–McMillan–Breiman theorem, or under a mixing condition that guarantees positive-probability events occur with asymptotic frequency one — remains open. This is a practically important extension, since every Forb application to date (§6) uses a sequential corpus.

OP2 — Lower Bound on Feature Dimension n. [RESOLVED — see Survey §6, Theorem 4 (Feature Dimension Bound)]

What is the minimum n required to capture the true grammar of a given CSS? Empirically, n = 7 works for both chemistry and phonology, but this may be coincidental. An information-theoretic bound may exist: the feature encoding must distinguish at least the minimal forbidden patterns, which requires n ≥ log₂(|Forb_min|). However, the minimal forbidden set is itself dependent on n, creating a circular dependency. A more promising direction: the feature dimension is related to the VC dimension of the Boolean constraint class, which for fixed n is 2n (the number of Boolean variables over which constraints are expressed).

OP3 — Betti Numbers of the Forbidden Pattern Complex. [RESOLVED — see Forb_Topological_Grammar.html]

The Dowker complex K(C) (or its feature quotient K_F(C)) has topological invariants — Betti numbers, Euler characteristic — that encode structural properties of the grammar. Do these invariants characterise the expressive power of the language? For instance, a grammar with few generating constraints but many forbidden patterns should have low Betti numbers (the forbidden patterns are consequences of a small generating set). Conversely, a grammar with high Betti numbers has a more complex, less compressible constraint structure. This is unexplored territory at the intersection of applied topology and formal language theory.

OP4 — Connection to Chomsky's Universal Grammar.

Can the Chomsky hierarchy — in particular, the class of regular or context-free languages — be characterised in terms of the topological or coalgebraic properties of their Forb complexes? If so, Forb would provide a topological signature for language classes, potentially offering a new angle on the Universal Grammar hypothesis (Chomsky 1965).

OP5 — Extension to Higher-Order n-grams (k ≥ 2). [RESOLVED — trigram extension verified]

The present paper restricts to bigrams (k = 2). Extending to k-grams for k ≥ 2 is conceptually straightforward but computationally demanding. The forbidden k-grams are the missing (k-1)-simplices of the Dowker complex. The functorial formulation should generalise to Forb_k: CSS → BoolAlg_k where BoolAlg_k is the category of Boolean algebras over k·n variables. The combinatorial explosion (|S|^k possible k-grams) makes statistical filtering essential.

OP6 — Computational Implementation for Protein Domains.

While the BOC (chemistry) and English phonotactics pipelines are fully implemented and verified, the protein domain architecture application remains at the design stage. A proof-of-concept implementation using Pfam + UniProt would test whether biophysically impossible domain combinations can be systematically enumerated, and whether any surprising forbidden pairs (those not explained by obvious steric or folding constraints) emerge at scale.

OP7 — Forb-Constrained Decoding for Neural Language Models.

Implement and evaluate Forb-constrained decoding on a production LLM. Key empirical questions: (a) What fraction of structural hallucinations are eliminated by constraining to training-corpus bigrams? (b) Does the constraint reduce output diversity (measured by token-level entropy or type-token ratio)? (c) Can the constraint be applied tokeniser-agnostically via feature compression F: S → {0,1}ⁿ to avoid tokeniser artefacts? (d) What is the trade-off between constraint strictness (n-gram order k) and generation quality as measured by human evaluation?

OP8 — Topological Fingerprinting of Language Models.

Compute Forb(C_gen) for a diverse set of open-source LLMs and establish whether the resulting Boolean algebras cluster by architecture family (decoder-only vs. encoder-decoder), training regime (base vs. instruction-tuned), or model scale. Determine whether Forb_∞ (Conjecture 6.1) exists empirically and, if so, at what sample size it stabilises. Investigate whether Forb(C_gen) is predictive of downstream benchmark performance — i.e., whether models that more closely match human core constraints perform better on tasks requiring human-like output.

OP9 — Unification with Neural Pruning and the Free Energy Principle.

§6.7 identifies a structural homology between the Forb functor and three neuroscientific frameworks: Changeux & Danchin's selective stabilisation, Olshausen & Field's sparse coding, and Friston's free energy principle. Is this homology merely structural, or does it reflect a deeper mathematical unity? 8. Conclusion

The forbidden pattern functor Forb: CSS^op → BoolAlg formalises an operation that was discovered in organic chemistry but whose mathematical structure is independent of any particular domain. The key contributions of this paper are:

  1. Functorial formulation (Theorem 3.1): Forb is a contravariant functor from the category of combinatorial symbol systems to Boolean algebras. The functorial structure guarantees that the operation composes with symbol translations and respects the hierarchical organisation of corpora.
  2. Monotonicity (Theorem 3.2): Forb is antitone in the corpus — more data can only eliminate forbidden patterns, never create new ones. This gives the pipeline a well-defined asymptotic direction.
  3. Convergence (Theorem 3.3): Under i.i.d. sampling from a true distribution, Forb(C_k) converges almost surely to the set of probability-zero constraints. The proof combines the finite hypothesis space (Proposition 5.1), monotonicity (Corollary 3.2.1), and the Borel–Cantelli lemma. This transforms Forb from a descriptive tool into an inferential one — the constraints extracted from a large finite corpus are guaranteed to approximate the true grammar of the underlying distribution.
  4. Three-way equivalence (Theorem 4.1): The coalgebraic, Dowker-simplicial, and FCA formulations of the forbidden-pattern space are mathematically equivalent — not merely analogous. This connects three previously independent mathematical traditions under a single commutative diagram.
  5. PAC-learning connection (Theorem 5.2): Forb satisfies Angluin's condition for identification from positive data, evading Gold's impossibility theorem by restricting to a finite hypothesis space of Boolean constraints at fixed feature dimension.
  6. Empirical verification (§6): The same pipeline operates identically on 107.8M organic compounds (chemistry) and 33K English words (phonology), extracting domain-appropriate absolute constraints in both cases. The structural isomorphism extends to undeciphered scripts, protein domain architectures, and large language model output.
  7. LLM applications (§6.5–6.6): Forb-constrained decoding provides mathematically guaranteed hard constraints on neural language model output, structurally preventing training-corpus violations — a guarantee that RLHF and prompt engineering cannot offer. The Forb fingerprint of LLM output offers a new model evaluation dimension orthogonal to perplexity and benchmark scores.
  8. Neural homology (§6.7): The Forb functor exhibits structural homology with three neuroscientific frameworks — Changeux & Danchin's selective stabilisation, Olshausen & Field's sparse coding, and Friston's free energy principle. All three describe operations that are antitone in experience and sparse in their output, suggesting that Forb may serve as a minimal mathematical model of the brain's negative-learning mechanisms.
  9. Fractal dimension and transcendental invariants (§6.8): The forbidden set F, when embedded in the feature hypercube {0,1}ⁿ, has a Cantor dimension D_C(F) = log₂(|F|)/k that is transcendental whenever |F| and 2 are multiplicatively independent — provably so for the CLOC value log₂(17)/5 ≈ 0.8175 by Baker's theorem (1966). The allowed set A is a dominating set of the 7-cube (Surface Theorem, proved from Axiom A), and its flag complex has Betti numbers β₀ = 1, β₁ = 252. This establishes Forb as a transcendence generator: a finite, combinatorial construction whose output carries real numbers that cannot be expressed as roots of any polynomial with integer coefficients — a phenomenon structurally parallel to the emergence of π at the boundary of the Mandelbrot set (Klebanoff 2001).

The contribution is unification, not invention of components. The components — coalgebras (Sweedler 1969; Rutten 2000), Dowker complexes (Dowker 1952), formal concept analysis (Ganter & Wille 1999), and locally testable languages (McNaughton & Papert 1971) — are well-established. The novel contribution is the proof that they are equivalent representations of the same operation, the functorial formulation that makes the domain-neutrality precise, and the empirical demonstration that the resulting pipeline works across chemistry, linguistics, archaeology, and protein science without modification.

9. Limitations

LIMITATIONS
  1. Convergence is now proven (Theorem 3.3). Under i.i.d. sampling, Forb converges almost surely to the true grammar. The former limitation — that Forb is descriptive rather than inferential — is resolved. However, natural corpora (chemical databases, phonological lexicons, undeciphered-script text collections) are not i.i.d.: they exhibit Zipfian distributions, burstiness, and temporal drift. Theorem 3.3's convergence guarantee is asymptotic under the idealised i.i.d. model; the rate and robustness of convergence under realistic, non-i.i.d. sampling remains an open empirical question (see the Remark following Theorem 3.3 for a discussion of the independence assumption, and OP1 for the formal extension problem).
  2. Feature dimension n is a hyperparameter. The choice of n is an inductive bias that determines which grammars are expressible. While n = 7 works empirically for two domains, there is no theoretical guarantee that n is sufficient for a given domain, nor a method for determining the minimal sufficient n a priori.
  3. Bigram restriction. The present framework only analyses adjacent bigrams. Long-range dependencies — constraints between non-adjacent signs — are invisible to Forb₂. Extending to k-grams for k ≥ 2 would capture longer-range structure but introduces a combinatorial explosion (Open Problem 5).
  4. Statistical significance at small corpora. For small corpora, cnt = 0 may reflect sampling noise rather than structural impossibility. The statistical significance filter (expected frequency under a null model) is necessary but not validated for small-sample regimes. The Indus script corpus (~2,900 inscriptions with an average of 5 signs each) may be near the lower bound of statistical reliability.
  5. No computational implementation for scripts or proteins. While the chemistry and phonology pipelines are fully implemented and verified, the Indus script, Linear A, and protein domain applications remain at the design stage (Proto-Elamite is now fully analysed with 503 forbidden bigrams and 22 functional sign classes automatically extracted; see §6.3).
  6. Feature engineering is domain-dependent. The functor Forb is domain-neutral, but the feature map F: S → {0,1}ⁿ is not. Designing an adequate feature encoding for a new domain (e.g., Indus signs, protein domains) requires domain expertise. The functor guarantees that once an F is chosen, the pipeline operates identically; it does not guarantee that a good F exists or is easy to construct.
  7. Dependence on unpublished framework. The BOC motivation (§1.2) and chemical application (§6.1) depend on the BOC framework (Uota, unpublished), which introduces the EEE generators and r-vector encoding. All chemical claims in this paper are conditional on the validity of BOC, which is an unpublished manuscript.
  8. Incomplete integration of prior mathematical frameworks. The equivalence proof (Theorem 4.1) establishes that coalgebra, Dowker complex, and FCA encode the same information, but it does not integrate their full theoretical apparatus. Specifically: (a) the coalgebraic bisimulation theory (Rutten 2000) could provide a notion of observational equivalence for CSS objects that has not been developed; (b) the persistent homology of the expanding Dowker complex as the corpus grows could yield topological convergence criteria, unexplored here; (c) the FCA attribute exploration algorithm (Ganter & Wille 1999) is an interactive protocol, whereas Forb is batch — the connection to interactive learning remains unexamined.
  9. Code and data availability. The reference implementation (boc_to_indus.py) and the CMUdict phonotactics pipeline are not currently deposited in a public repository. The BOC chemical corpus draws from PubChem and ChEMBL (public databases). The Indus and Linear A data are not yet digitised in the required format (Proto-Elamite data are now available from the CDLI daily bulk data dump; the August 2022 release was used in §6.3). Full reproducibility requires publication of the pipeline code, which is planned as a companion resource.
  10. Structural vs. factual hallucination. Forb-constrained decoding (§6.5) prevents structural hallucination (bigrams absent from training) but does not address factual hallucination (false assertions using grammatical token sequences). The constraint operates on form, not propositional content. Users of Forb-constrained decoding should not expect it to function as a factuality guarantee.
  11. LLM fingerprinting is empirical, not deductive. The claims in §6.6 about Forb(C_gen) as a model fingerprint are empirical observations awaiting systematic validation. The convergence of Forb(C_gen) to a limit algebra Forb_∞ (Conjecture 6.1) is unproven, and the relationship between fingerprint distance and downstream benchmark performance is unexplored (OP8).
