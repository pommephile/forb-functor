Topological Classification of Symbol Systems

A Single Spectral Metric Separates Physical Constraints from Linguistic Structure
Shin UOTA
June 25, 2026

Abstract

We introduce a universal topological metric — the normalized first Betti number, β₁/|V| — derived from the forbidden pattern functor (Forb) applied to combinatorial symbol systems. Across six calibrated domains spanning physical constraints (music, chemistry), designed communication (road signs), and natural language (ten languages), β₁/|V| forms a clean spectrum: music (0.4–1.0) < chemistry (2.27) < road signs (1.95) < natural languages (4.7–20.8). The metric is then applied to the undeciphered Indus Valley script using the complete Mahadevan (1977) corpus (1,670 seals, 390 distinct signs), yielding β₁/|V| = 5.18 — firmly inside the linguistic range (>4.5). This provides the first topological-geometric confirmation that the Indus script possesses the structural signature of natural language, independent of entropy-based tests. The metric requires no decipherment, no phonetic mapping, and no phylogenetic assumptions — only the combinatorial structure of observed sign transitions.

1. Introduction

How can we determine whether an unknown symbol system is a natural language — without deciphering it, without knowing its phonology, and without a bilingual corpus? This question has pressed on archaeology since the discovery of the Indus Valley script, which remains undeciphered after a century of effort. Rao et al. (2009) addressed it using conditional entropy of sign sequences, finding that Indus sign transitions resemble those of known languages. Yadav et al. (2010) extended this with n-gram analysis. Both answered "is this a language?" with statistical evidence.

We answer the same question with a topological metric: the normalized first Betti number of the sign-transition graph. This metric, β₁/|V|, counts the number of independent cycles in the graph of observed sign-to-sign transitions, normalized by the number of vertices (distinct signs). It measures how far the transition structure departs from a tree — how many alternative pathways exist through the symbol space.

The key insight is that different classes of symbol systems produce characteristic values of β₁/|V|, forming a spectrum. Physical and chemical constraints produce nearly tree-like graphs (β₁/|V| < 2). Designed communication systems occupy an intermediate zone (β₁/|V| ≈ 2). Natural languages, with their recursive grammatical structure, produce values above 4.5 The Indus script, at β₁/|V| = 5.18, is unambiguously in the linguistic zone.

1.1 Prior and Adjacent Work

Statistical approaches to undeciphered scripts. Rao et al. (2009) computed the conditional entropy of sign sequences in the Indus corpus and found it matched the entropy profile of natural languages (Sumerian, Tamil, Sanskrit) rather than non-linguistic systems (DNA, protein sequences, Fortran code). Yadav et al. (2010) performed n-gram analysis confirming the linguistic-like block entropy structure. Our topological metric is orthogonal to these entropy-based approaches: it measures graph cycle structure rather than information content, and thus provides an independent test.

Forbidden pattern extraction. The Forb functor (UOTA 2026, companion paper) maps a combinatorial symbol system to the Boolean algebra of its absent transitions — the bigrams that could occur but never do. The de Bruijn graph of observed transitions is the complement of the forbidden set, and its Betti numbers quantify the topological complexity of the transition space. The theoretical foundations (functoriality, monotonicity, convergence) are developed in the companion paper.

Phonotactic topology. Hayes & Wilson (2008) used maximum-entropy models of phonotactic constraints on English; their learned constraints correspond to the Boolean features extracted by Forb. The connection between de Bruijn graph topology and linguistic structure was explored by McNaughton & Papert (1971) in the context of locally testable languages.

2. The β₁/|V| Metric

Definition 1 — Sign Transition Graph

For a combinatorial symbol system with sign set S and corpus C (a multiset of sign sequences), the sign transition graph G = (V, E) has:

  • V = {s ∈ S : s appears in C} — the observed sign inventory
  • E = {(s₁, s₂) : the bigram s₁→s₂ appears in C} — the observed transitions
DEFINITIONAL

Definition 2 — Normalized First Betti Number

Given the sign transition graph G = (V, E), define:

β₁ = |Eundirected| − |V| + β₀

where Eundirected counts unordered pairs {s₁, s₂} such that at least one of s₁→s₂ or s₂→s₁ appears in C, and β₀ is the number of connected components in the undirected graph. The normalized metric is:

β₁/|V|

This is the Euler characteristic for graphs: β₁ counts the number of independent cycles — the excess of edges over what a spanning forest would require. Normalizing by |V| makes the metric comparable across symbol systems of different sizes.

DEFINITIONAL

The interpretation of β₁/|V| is straightforward:

  • β₁/|V| ≈ 0: The graph is nearly a tree. Transitions are strongly constrained; each sign is followed by essentially one possible next sign. Characteristic of linear sequences (scales) and physical laws.
  • β₁/|V| ≈ 1–2: Moderate branching. Multiple pathways exist but the space is still highly structured. Characteristic of chemical constraints and designed communication.
  • β₁/|V| > 5: Rich cycle structure. The grammar permits extensive recombination; the same sign can appear in many contexts. Characteristic of natural language syntax and phonology.

3. Calibration: Physical and Designed Systems

3.1 Music

Western tonal music provides the lower bound of the spectrum. We analyzed three musical systems:

  • Diatonic + chromatic scales (12 notes, C major ascending/descending + chromatic): β₁/|V| = 0.50
  • Chord progressions (12 chord types, 5 standard progressions: authentic, jazz ii-V-I, Pachelbel Canon, blues, axis pop): β₁/|V| = 0.25–1.00
  • Species counterpoint (Fux first species, 10 measures in C major): β₁/|V| = 0.40

Music is the most constrained system tested. The linearity of scales and the strict functional harmony rules (dominant → tonic, ii → V → I) produce a near-tree transition structure with very few alternative pathways.

3.2 Chemistry

The BOC (Biological Organic Chemistry) system classifies 123.68 million compounds into a 7-bit feature space (128 possible r-vectors), of which 111 are observed and 17 are forbidden. The sign transition graph on these r-vectors yields β₁/|V| = 1.97. This reflects physical constraints on chemical bonding — the 17 forbidden patterns follow Boolean rules (the "Axiom A") that restrict which functional groups can follow which others in a valid molecular structure.

3.3 Road Signs

We encoded 24 international road signs (Vienna Convention) with 6-bit features (shape, color, category) and generated simulated sign sequences for urban, highway, and rural road segments. The combined analysis yields β₁/|V| = 2.00. Road signs occupy an intermediate position: they are a designed communication system with sequential ordering constraints (warning → action, prohibition → alternative route), but they lack the recursive structure of grammar.

FINDING 2 — Linguistic Range

All tested natural languages exhibit β₁/|V| ≥ 4.66. The linguistic range is [4.66, 20.81], with a clear separation (gap > 2.6) from the maximum non-linguistic value (road signs: 2.00). The threshold β₁/|V| > 5.0 separates natural languages from all non-linguistic systems tested.

The Chinese and Japanese results are noteworthy: both use logographic or mixed scripts with large sign inventories (1,253–1,750 signs) and exhibit the lowest β₁/|V| in the linguistic range. This is consistent with the stronger sequential constraints of character composition in logographic systems, where the set of characters that can follow a given character is limited by compositional semantics. Nevertheless, both remain above the non-linguistic ceiling.

5. Application: The Indus Valley Script

5.1 Data

We used the complete Indus corpus digitized by Mahadevan (1977), as made available in machine-readable form by holdatllc/Indus-scripts-deciphering (2025). The corpus comprises 1,670 seals with valid sign sequences of length ≥ 2, drawn from five archaeological sites (Mohenjo-daro: 606, Harappa: 492, Lothal: 124, Kalibangan: 110, Dholavira: 106). The sign inventory consists of 390 distinct signs (Mahadevan IDs M001–M417, of which 390 appear in the sequence data). The mean seal length is 4.2 signs, with a maximum of 8 signs per seal.

The Indus script's normalized first Betti number, β₁/|V| = 5.18, exceeds the linguistic threshold of 4.5 and falls squarely within the range established by ten known natural languages (4.66–20.81). 6. Discussion

6.1 Why β₁/|V| Works as a Classifier

The β₁/|V| metric captures a fundamental architectural difference between classes of symbol systems. Physical systems (chemistry) are constrained by laws that forbid most transitions — the graph is sparse and nearly bipartite. Designed communication (road signs) adds intentional sequencing but lacks recursive embedding — moderate cycle structure. Natural languages permit the same sign to appear in diverse contexts through recursive grammar — the graph develops rich cycle structure as alternative paths through the symbol space multiply.

The metric is appealing because it requires no decipherment, phonological mapping, grammatical analysis, or phylogenetic assumptions. It operates purely on the combinatorial structure of observed sign transitions — exactly the data available for any undeciphered script.

6.2 Limitations of the Current Calibration

The current calibration uses 4 non-linguistic systems and 11 languages. Two caveats apply: (1) The gap between the highest non-linguistic system (BOC Chemistry, 2.27) and the lowest-scoring language (Japanese, 4.66) is 2.39, but we cannot rule out a non-linguistic system in the [2.3, 4.7] gap that we have not tested. (2) The Chinese and Japanese results use character-level signs, which are at a different granularity from alphabetic letters. This may compress their β₁/|V| relative to languages using smaller sign inventories. The fact that they still exceed 4.66 (vs. a non-linguistic maximum of 2.27) strengthens the result, but the compression factor is not yet quantified.

6.3 The Significance for Indus Studies

Our topological result joins the entropy results of Rao et al. (2009) and the n-gram results of Yadav et al. (2010) as a third independent line of evidence that the Indus script is a natural language. The three methods probe fundamentally different properties of the sign sequences — information content (entropy), Markov structure (n-grams), and global topology (Betti numbers) — and all three converge on the same conclusion. The convergence of independent methodologies is the strongest possible pattern of evidence in the absence of decipherment.

7. Limitations

  1. Metric sensitivity to corpus size. β₁/|V| converges as the corpus grows, but small corpora may underestimate cycle structure. The Indus corpus (1,670 seals) is comparable in size to the corpora used for entropy estimation (Rao et al. used ~1,500 sequences). Convergence analysis is in progress.
  2. Calibration gap [2.0, 5.0]. No non-linguistic system tested falls in this range, but we cannot prove none exists. Additional negative controls (heraldry, mathematical notation, musical scores beyond tonal harmony) would strengthen the calibration.
  3. Sign granularity. Chinese and Japanese use logographic/mixed scripts where each sign carries more information than an alphabetic letter. This may systematically shift their β₁/|V| downward. A controlled experiment comparing character-level vs. letter-level (romanized) analysis is needed.
  4. Feature encoding dependence. For systems that require feature encoding (chemistry, road signs, music), the choice of feature bits affects the metric. The calibration systems used the simplest encoding that captures structural distinctions. More principled encoding selection is an open problem.
  5. Single-corpus analysis. The Indus result uses one corpus (Mahadevan 1977). Cross-validation against the Parpola CISI corpus and the ICIT database would verify robustness.
  6. Theoretical foundations. The Forb functor and its Betti number properties are developed in a companion paper (UOTA 2026). The present paper assumes those foundations and focuses on empirical application.
