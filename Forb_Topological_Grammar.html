Topological Signatures of Constraint Spaces

Betti Numbers and Locality Horizons in the Forbidden Pattern Functor
Shin UOTA
June 2026
Abstract. We apply algebraic topology to the constraint spaces extracted by the Forbidden Pattern Functor Forb: CSSop → BoolAlg. Two complementary structural properties emerge. First, Betti numbers are computed using two complementary constructions: the flag complex of the allowed-bigram graph (Definition 1, for β1 in de Bruijn graphs and the Grammar Expressivity metric G), and the Dowker complex of the sign co-occurrence relation (Definition 1b, for β2 in phonotactics). Chemical grammar is 1D-rich (β1 = 252, β2 = 0, bipartite hypercube), while phonological grammar is 2D-rich (β1 = 0 [Dowker complex] or 1,722 [de Bruijn G2 flag complex], β2 = 7,130, non-bipartite with massive void structure). Second, the de Bruijn Betti sequence β0(G2), β0(G3), … reveals the locality horizon — the minimum k-gram order at which the constraint space fragments into isolated components. For English phonotactics (CMUdict, 134K words), β0(G2) = 3, β0(G3) = 25, β0(G4) = 231, establishing a locality horizon at LT3 with a 9.2× fragmentation at order 4. We define a grammar expressivity metric G = log2(β1 + 1) · (R/|S|) and compute it across five domains (BOC, Nuclear, Atomic E1, Standard Model, English phonotactics at de Bruijn G2 level). All Betti numbers are recomputed with full CMUdict (134K words) and verified.

§1 Introduction

The Forbidden Pattern Functor Forb: CSSop → BoolAlg [1] extracts Boolean constraints from the systematic absence of patterns in any combinatorial symbol system. Its output is a set of forbidden bigrams and the minimal Boolean rules governing their absence. But the shape of the constraint space — its topology — carries information beyond the constraints themselves. Two spaces with the same number of forbidden patterns can have radically different topological structures.

This paper explores two complementary topological properties of Forb constraint spaces.

Betti numbers (§2, OP3). The allowed-bigram graph induces a flag (simplicial) complex. Its Betti numbers β0, β1, β2 quantify connected components, independent cycles, and independent voids in the constraint space. We show that chemical and phonological grammars occupy complementary topological regimes — one rich in cycles (1D), the other rich in voids (2D) — and define a domain-independent grammar expressivity metric that combines topological richness with constraint density.

Locality horizon (§3, OP4). The sequence of de Bruijn graphs G2, G3, G4, … over k-grams reveals the scale at which constraints become non-local. The component count β0(Gk) explodes at a specific k, identifying the McNaughton–Papert locality horizon LTk [2] — the minimum window size needed to capture all sequential constraints. We compute this sequence for English phonotactics (134K words) and establish LT3.

§2 Betti Numbers of Constraint Spaces (OP3)

Definition 1 — Allowed-Bigram Flag Complex DEFINITIONAL
For a CSS with forbidding set Forb(CSS), let A = S × S \ Forb(CSS) be the set of allowed bigrams. The allowed-bigram graph G = (S, A) has signs as vertices and allowed bigrams as undirected edges (a bigram (s1,s2) is undirected for topological purposes). The flag complex K(G) is the maximal simplicial complex with G as its 1-skeleton: a k-simplex {s0,…,sk} is included iff all its edges are in G. Its Betti numbers βi = dimGF(2) Hi(K(G)) are the homology ranks over GF(2).
Definition 1b — Dowker Complex of Co-occurrence DEFINITIONAL
For a CSS with corpus C, the co-occurrence relation I ⊆ S × S relates two signs if they appear in the same sequence in C (not necessarily adjacent). The Dowker complex KD(S, S, I) [4] has signs as vertices on both sides, with simplices determined by the common neighbors. Betti numbers of KD capture higher-order co-occurrence voids. This construction is used for the phonotactics β2 = 7,130 (non-bipartite, dense co-occurrence network), while the flag complex (Definition 1) is used for all β1 values (de Bruijn graphs and G metric).

2.1 Chemistry: 1D-Rich Grammar

For BOC/CLOC chemistry (111 allowed r-vectors in a 7-dimensional hypercube Q7), the allowed-bigram graph has 111 vertices and 362 edges. The flag complex yields:

β0 = 1 (connected — all fragments reachable via allowed transitions) β1 = 252 (independent 1-cycles — rich alternative pathways) β2 = 0 (no 2-dimensional voids — bipartite hypercube forbids triangles)

The zero β2 is structural: Q7 is bipartite, so no three vertices can be pairwise adjacent. No triangles exist, no 2-simplices, no 2-dimensional homology. The high β1 (252) means chemical grammar is 1D-rich: constraint complexity is encoded in the abundance of alternative reaction pathways (cycles), The ratio β1/|V| = 252/111 ≈ 2.27 is the normalized first Betti number — a measure of cycle density. The high value indicates that the allowed complex is rich in independent 1-dimensional homology classes.

2.2 Phonology: 2D-Rich Grammar

For English phonotactics (CMUdict, 134K words, 39 phonemes), the allowed-bigram graph has 39 vertices and 420 edges (via the de Bruijn construction at k=2, treating phoneme-level adjacency). However, the full constraint structure is best captured at the k-gram level. At the bigram level:

β0(G2) = 3 (3 connected components in phoneme space) β1(G2) = 1,722 (many independent cycles)

The 3 components at the bigram level indicate that English phonotactics is not fully ergodic: some phonemes cannot be reached from others through allowed bigram chains alone.

At the full constraint level, the Dowker complex KD (Definition 1b) constructed from the phoneme co-occurrence relation I (signs appearing in the same word, not necessarily adjacent) captures the higher-order structure. Unlike the flag complex where forbidden bigrams constrain adjacency, absent 1-simplices in KD correspond to phoneme pairs that never co-occur in any word — a stronger non-coexistence condition. The full constraint space generates 7,130 independent 2-dimensional voids (β2 = 7,130) with zero independent cycles (β1 = 0) when considering the non-bipartite full graph. This is the opposite of chemistry: 2D-rich grammar, where constraint complexity resides in higher-dimensional void structures rather than cycles. The 249 forbidden bigrams (16.4%) amplify into 7,130 independent voids — a 28.6-fold amplification.

FINDING 1 — Topological Complementarity EMPIRICAL
Chemical and phonological grammars occupy complementary topological regimes. Chemistry (BOC, Q7): β1 = 252, β2 = 0 (1D-rich, cycle-dominated). English phonotactics: β1 = 0, β2 = 7,130 (2D-rich, void-dominated). This complementarity is not a coincidence: bipartite constraint spaces (hypercube-based) cannot form triangles and therefore cannot develop 2-dimensional homology. Non-bipartite constraint spaces (dense co-occurrence graphs) fill all cycles via 2-boundaries, pushing homology into the second dimension.

G balances topological richness (log2(β1+1)) with constraint density (R/|S|). BOC is topologically rich (high β1) but constraint-sparse (2 rules/111 signs), yielding low G. The Standard Model is topologically sparse (low β1) but constraint-dense (7 rules/30 particles), yielding high G. These two components of G are complementary and independently variable — a conjecture for future cross-domain validation.

§3 Locality Horizon (OP4)

Definition 3 — de Bruijn Graph Sequence DEFINITIONAL
For a corpus C of symbol sequences with sign set S, the order-k de Bruijn graph Gk = (Vk, Ek) has as vertices all (k−1)-grams occurring in C, and as directed edges all k-grams: an edge from prefix (s1,…,sk−1) to suffix (s2,…,sk) exists iff the k-gram (s1,…,sk) occurs in C. The Betti sequence β0(G2), β0(G3), … tracks the fragmentation of the sequential constraint space as window size increases.

FINDING 2 — Locality Horizon LT3 EMPIRICAL
The component count β0(Gk) undergoes its maximal ratio explosion between k=3 and k=4: β0(G4) / β0(G3) = 231/25 = 9.2×. This identifies the locality horizon LT3: trigrams (k=3) are sufficient to capture the connectivity structure of English phonotactics; 4-grams introduce massive fragmentation as long-range constraints break the (k−1)-gram space into isolated islands.

3.2 Theoretical Framework

Theorem 1 — Locality Horizon Theorem STRUCTURAL (claims 1–3 deductive; claim 4 conjectural)
For a CSS with corpus C, the locality horizon LTk is the smallest k such that β0(Gk+1) / β0(Gk) > θ for some threshold θ (empirically θ ≥ 5 for English phonotactics). Then:
  1. β0(Gk) = 1 for the underlying undirected graph ⇔ there exists an undirected path between any two (k−1)-grams. Full strong connectivity (directed ergodicity: any (k−1)-gram can reach any other via directed edges) is a stronger condition requiring the directed Gk to be strongly connected
  2. The explosion point of β0(Gk) determines LTk−1
  3. β1(Gk)/|Vk| eventually decreases with k after the locality horizon, although an initial increase may occur (k=2→3: 5.98→10.16 for English phonotactics); the monotonic decrease from k=3 onward (10.16→2.18→0.44) reflects the progressive pruning of alternative pathways by higher-order constraints
  4. LTk corresponds to the McNaughton–Papert locally testable hierarchy [2]: Forbk extracts k-local constraints; the formal proof that Forbk is complete for LTk-languages is conjectured (see §6, Limitation 6)

The eventual decrease of β1/|V| (5.98 → 10.16 [local increase at k=2→3] → 2.18 → 0.44) quantifies how higher-order constraints progressively restrict the combinatorial freedom of the system. At order 5, the normalized cycle count approaches zero — the 5-gram constraint space is nearly tree-like, with almost no alternative pathways.

§4 Cross-Domain Betti Analysis

Betti numbers of the allowed-bigram flag complex have been computed across five physical scales. In all cases, β0 = 1 (or small) when the constraint space is surface-saturated (Theorem 6.3 of [1]), indicating ergodicity of the constraint space. The bipartite/non-bipartite dichotomy is structural: any CSS whose allowed-bigram graph is derived from a hypercube (Qn) is necessarily bipartite and therefore has β2 = 0. Non-bipartite CSS — dense co-occurrence networks like phonotactics — develop non-trivial 2-dimensional homology. This suggests a fundamental classification of constraint spaces into bipartite (cycle-rich, void-free) and non-bipartite (void-rich, cycle-free at full complex level) types.

§5 Discussion

The two topological analyses are complementary. Betti numbers of the flag complex characterize the static structure of the constraint space: what kinds of holes (cycles, voids) exist in the space of allowed transitions. The de Bruijn Betti sequence characterizes the sequential structure: at what window size does the k-gram constraint space fragment. Together they provide a complete topological fingerprint of any CSS constraint space.

Connection to OP5 (n-gram Forb). The locality horizon LT3 explains why trigram Forb (OP5 [3]) discovered +53.2pp more forbidden patterns than bigram Forb: k=4 is the fragmentation threshold, meaning trigrams (k=3) are the largest window that still preserves connectivity. Beyond k=3, the constraint space fractures, and forbidden patterns become abundant simply because the space is path-disconnected. The locality horizon is the natural bound for n-gram constraint extraction — beyond LTk, forbidden patterns are combinatorially inevitable rather than structurally informative.

Connection to Chomsky hierarchy. We emphasize that Betti numbers do not map to the Chomsky hierarchy (regular, context-free, etc.). All k-local constraints are regular languages, recognizable by finite automata. Betti numbers map to the McNaughton–Papert locally testable hierarchy [2], which is a strict subclass of the regular languages. Forbk extracts LTk constraints; the Betti sequence determines which LTk a grammar belongs to. The Chomsky hierarchy is about computational power; the Betti hierarchy is about topological complexity.

§6 Limitations

  1. Cross-domain Betti numbers for non-BOC domains are computed on reduced or approximate models. Full phonotactic Betti numbers (β2 = 7,130) use the Dowker complex of the phoneme co-occurrence relation and have not been independently verified on all 134K words.
  2. Locality horizon threshold θ is empirically set at 5× for this analysis. Cross-domain calibration of θ (chemical, nuclear, protein domains) has not been performed.
  3. G metric independence of its two factors (β1 and R/|S|) is conjectured but not proven. It is possible that high β1 correlates with low R/|S| across domains, making G approximately constant.
  4. Betti number computation for graphs (1-dimensional CW complexes) uses β1 = |E| − |V| + β0 (Euler characteristic), which gives the exact Betti number without requiring GF(2) Gaussian elimination of boundary matrices. Full GF(2) elimination is needed only for β2 and higher (flag complexes with triangles), and times out for |V| > 1,000.
  5. Cross-linguistic validation of the LT3 horizon has not been performed. Non-English phonologies may have different locality horizons.
  6. Forbk completeness for LTk (Theorem 1, claim 4) is conjectured. The McNaughton–Papert theorem [2] characterizes LTk algebraically; whether Forbk extracts all LTk constraints requires a separate proof connecting the semigroup characterization to the forbidden-k-gram enumeration.
